# Pug History: From Chinese Emperors to Modern Companion — 15 Facts You Didn't Know

Few dogs carry as much history in those wrinkled faces as the pug.

Over 2,000 years of documented existence. Royal palaces on three continents. Assassination plots thwarted. Wedding night bites inflicted on Napoleon Bonaparte. The pug's history is stranger, richer, and more dramatic than most people realize.

Here are 15 facts that reveal the real story of this extraordinary breed.

---

## 1. Pugs Are One of the Oldest Dog Breeds in the World

The pug originated in **China around 200 BCE**, during the Han Dynasty. That makes them over 2,000 years old — older than most countries, and older than almost every other dog breed alive today.

They were developed alongside two other flat-faced breeds still recognizable today: the Shih Tzu and the Pekingese. All three were bred not for hunting, herding, or guarding — but purely for companionship.

---

## 2. Their Wrinkles Were Bred to Resemble Chinese Royalty

This wasn't an accident or a quirk. Chinese breeders deliberately cultivated the distinctive forehead wrinkles because the folds resembled the Chinese character for **"prince"** (王, wáng).

To own a pug with perfect prince markings was considered auspicious. It connected the dog — and by extension, the owner — to royalty and good fortune.

---

## 3. They Were Guarded By Soldiers

Chinese emperors didn't just love their pugs — they treated them as living treasures. Imperial pugs were kept in extraordinary luxury, fed delicacies, and given their own personal guards.

Only emperors and their immediate families were permitted to own pugs. Commoners caught owning one faced severe punishment. For centuries, the pug was the most exclusive dog in the world.

---

## 4. A Pug Saved the Prince of Orange From Assassination

In 1572, during the Dutch revolt against Spain, a pug named **Pompey** allegedly saved the life of William I, Prince of Orange (William the Silent), by alerting him to the approach of Spanish assassins in the night.

Whether the story is entirely accurate or somewhat embellished by history, the effect was real: **the pug became the official dog of the Dutch royal House of Orange**, a distinction it holds symbolically to this day.

---

## 5. Dutch Traders Brought Pugs to Europe

Following trade routes from China to the Netherlands in the 1500s, Dutch merchants brought pugs back to Europe. The breed spread rapidly through the courts of Europe, favored by royalty who saw them as status symbols of the highest order.

The Dutch called them **"mopshond"** — a name still used in the Netherlands today.

---

## 6. William and Mary Introduced Pugs to England

When **William III and Mary II** of Orange took the English throne in 1689, they brought their pugs with them. The Dutch royal court's love of pugs transferred to English aristocracy almost immediately.

Within years, pugs were ubiquitous in wealthy English households. Portraits from the period show lords, ladies, and their pugs in nearly equal prominence.

---

## 7. Napoleon's Wife Had a Pug That Bit Him on Their Wedding Night

**Josephine Bonaparte** had a pug named **Fortune** whom she adored. Fortune slept in Josephine's bed — including on her wedding night with Napoleon.

Napoleon, apparently not a dog person (or at least not a *this particular dog* person), was bitten by Fortune when he attempted to reclaim his side of the bed. The mark was reportedly visible the next morning.

Fortune also served as an unlikely courier — Josephine reportedly hid messages under his collar to communicate with her family while imprisoned during the Reign of Terror.

---

## 8. Queen Victoria Was Obsessed With Pugs

**Queen Victoria** owned dozens of pugs throughout her reign and was instrumental in standardizing the breed in England. She was a founding supporter of the Kennel Club (established 1873) and used her influence to shape the breed's official standards.

Victoria preferred the fawn-colored pug and is credited with helping establish that as the dominant color in British breeding lines.

---

## 9. The Word "Pug" May Come From a Latin Term for "Fist"

The etymology of the word "pug" is debated, but one leading theory is that it derives from the Latin **pugnus**, meaning "fist" — a reference to the clenched-fist appearance of the pug's flat face in profile.

Another theory connects it to an Old English word for a small demon or spirit — which, honestly, tracks with pug personality.

---

## 10. A Group of Pugs Is Called a "Grumble"

The official collective noun for a group of pugs is a **grumble** — apparently named for the snorting, snuffling sounds they make. Alternative terms occasionally used include a "frown" of pugs or a "mop" of pugs.

"Grumble" is widely considered the most accurate.

---

## 11. The Breed's Official Motto Is "Multum in Parvo"

Latin for **"a lot in a little"**, this motto has been associated with pugs for centuries. It captures something essential about the breed: a full-sized personality, loyalty, and heart packed into a compact, 14-pound body.

The phrase appears in breed standards and is considered part of the breed's official identity.

---

## 12. Pugs Were Painted by the Masters

The pug's cultural significance in 18th-century Europe is visible in art. **William Hogarth**, the famous English painter and satirist, owned a pug named Trump and included pugs in multiple major works. His 1745 self-portrait, *The Painter and His Pug*, features Trump prominently.

Other painters who depicted pugs include Francisco Goya and many court portraitists across Europe.

---

## 13. The AKC Recognized Pugs in 1885

The American Kennel Club officially recognized the pug in **1885**, making it one of the earliest breeds recognized by the AKC. The breed is classified in the Toy Group.

The Pug Dog Club of America was founded in 1931 and remains active today.

---

## 14. Pugs Have Had Some Famous Modern Owners

Beyond historical royalty, pugs have attracted celebrity owners in modern times:
- **Rob Zombie** — rock musician and longtime pug enthusiast
- **Paris Hilton** — owned a black pug named Little Man
- **Gerard Butler** — photographed regularly with his pug
- **Jessica Alba**, **Hugh Laurie**, and **Billy Joel** have all been associated with the breed

In pop culture, pugs appear in *Men in Black* (Frank the Pug), *The Adventures of Milo and Otis*, and numerous advertisements.

---

## 15. The Pug Is One of the Most Popular Breeds in the World

Despite (or perhaps because of) their very specific needs, pugs consistently rank among the most popular dog breeds globally. In the United Kingdom, they regularly appear in the top 10. In the United States, they rank in the top 30 of AKC registrations annually.

Their appeal crosses every demographic: they work equally well for retirees, young professionals, families, and everyone in between. The combination of low exercise requirements, apartment suitability, affectionate temperament, and comedic personality makes them uniquely versatile.

---

## The Pug Today

Two thousand years on, the pug has changed very little in its essential nature. It remains what it was designed to be: a companion dog of extraordinary devotion and personality.

The same dog that slept at the feet of Chinese emperors, saved Dutch princes, bit Napoleon, and charmed Queen Victoria is now curled up on sofas in apartments from Tokyo to Toronto, snoring loudly and demanding attention.

Some designs are simply perfect.
