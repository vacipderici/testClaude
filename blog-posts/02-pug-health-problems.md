# 7 Common Pug Health Problems (And How to Prevent Them)

Pugs are joyful, loving dogs — but they come with a health profile unlike most breeds. Their distinctive flat face, bulging eyes, and compact body create a set of vulnerabilities that every owner should understand.

The good news: most pug health problems are manageable with the right knowledge, a proactive vet relationship, and good daily habits. Here are the seven most common issues and what you can do about each.

---

## 1. Brachycephalic Airway Syndrome (BAS)

**What it is:** The most significant health concern for pugs. BAS is a group of upper airway abnormalities caused by the compressed skull structure. It can include:
- **Stenotic nares** — narrow, pinched nostrils
- **Elongated soft palate** — tissue that partially blocks the airway
- **Hypoplastic trachea** — a trachea narrower than normal
- **Everted laryngeal saccules** — tissue pulled into the airway

**Warning signs:**
- Loud snoring (normal in pugs, but severe snoring warrants evaluation)
- Noisy, labored breathing at rest
- Gagging or retching
- Exercise intolerance — tiring after minimal activity
- Blue or pale gums (emergency)

**Prevention and management:**
- Keep your pug at a healthy weight — obesity dramatically worsens BAS
- Avoid exercise in heat or humidity
- Use a harness instead of a collar
- Ask your vet about a BAS evaluation — surgical correction (widening nostrils, trimming the soft palate) is common and significantly improves quality of life

## 2. Eye Problems

**What it is:** Pugs have very shallow eye sockets, causing their eyes to protrude. This makes them vulnerable to:
- **Corneal ulcers** — scratches or abrasions on the eye surface
- **Dry eye (KCS)** — insufficient tear production
- **Proptosis** — the eye partially or fully displaces from the socket
- **Entropion** — eyelid rolls inward, causing lashes to scratch the eye

**Warning signs:**
- Redness or cloudiness of the eye
- Excessive discharge or tearing
- Pawing at the eye
- Squinting or keeping one eye closed
- Visible white of the eye (sclera) more than usual

**Prevention and management:**
- Daily eye wipe with a warm, damp cloth
- Keep lubricating eye drops on hand (vet-recommended brands)
- **Proptosis is an emergency** — if an eye appears to be coming out of the socket, get to a vet immediately. Time matters.
- Avoid rough play with larger dogs that could cause facial trauma
- Never use a neck collar — stick to harnesses

## 3. Obesity

**What it is:** Pugs are food-driven and have low exercise tolerance. This combination makes obesity extremely common — and extremely dangerous. Extra weight puts pressure on already compromised airways, strains joints, and worsens every other health condition.

**Warning signs:**
- Can't feel the ribs without pressing firmly
- No visible waist when viewed from above
- Reluctance to exercise (beyond normal pug laziness)
- Increased breathing difficulty

**Prevention and management:**
- Measure every meal — never free-feed
- Limit treats to 10% or less of daily calories
- Use low-calorie training treats
- Two short walks daily
- If your pug is overweight, work with your vet on a weight loss plan rather than crash dieting

## 4. Skin Fold Dermatitis

**What it is:** Bacteria and yeast accumulate in the warm, moist skin folds of a pug's face (and sometimes tail area). Without daily cleaning, this causes painful infections.

**Warning signs:**
- Redness inside the wrinkles
- Unpleasant smell from the face
- Your pug rubbing their face on the floor or carpet
- Discharge or crust inside folds

**Prevention and management:**
- Clean wrinkles **daily** with a damp cloth or dog-safe wipe
- Dry thoroughly after cleaning — moisture is the problem
- Mild infections: vet-prescribed topical cream or wipe
- Severe or recurring infections: your vet may recommend surgical removal of deep folds (a safe, routine procedure)

## 5. Dental Disease

**What it is:** A pug's small jaw packs a full set of teeth into limited space, causing crowding. Crowded teeth trap plaque and tartar, leading to periodontal disease, tooth loss, and — in severe cases — bacteria entering the bloodstream and affecting organs.

Studies show that **80% of dogs have some degree of dental disease by age 3**. In flat-faced breeds, it develops faster.

**Warning signs:**
- Bad breath
- Difficulty eating or dropping food
- Visible brown buildup on teeth
- Swollen or bleeding gums
- Pawing at the mouth

**Prevention and management:**
- Brush teeth **daily** — this is the single most effective intervention
- Use dog-safe toothpaste only (never human toothpaste)
- Annual professional dental cleanings under anesthesia starting at age 1–2
- Dental chews can supplement but do not replace brushing

## 6. Ear Infections

**What it is:** Pugs have narrow ear canals that trap moisture, debris, and wax. Combined with the skin folds around the face, they're prone to recurring ear infections (otitis externa).

**Warning signs:**
- Head shaking or tilting
- Scratching at one or both ears
- Dark brown or black discharge
- Unpleasant odor from the ear
- Redness or swelling of the outer ear

**Prevention and management:**
- Clean ears every 1–2 weeks with a vet-approved ear cleaning solution
- Dry ears thoroughly after bathing or swimming
- Never use cotton swabs inside the ear canal
- Recurring infections may indicate allergies — ask your vet about an allergy workup

## 7. Heatstroke

**What it is:** Because pugs can't pant efficiently, they overheat rapidly in warm or humid conditions. Heatstroke is life-threatening and can develop within minutes.

**Warning signs:**
- Heavy, rapid panting
- Drooling excessively
- Bright red gums
- Staggering or disorientation
- Vomiting
- Collapse

**What to do in a heatstroke emergency:**
1. Move your pug to a cool, shaded area immediately
2. Apply cool (not ice cold) water to their body — especially paws, armpits, and groin
3. Offer small sips of water if they can swallow
4. Call your vet or emergency animal hospital — do not wait to see if they improve

**Prevention:**
- Never exercise in temperatures above 75°F (24°C)
- **Never leave a pug in a parked car** — not even for two minutes
- Always carry water on walks
- Provide air conditioning during warm months — this is not optional for pugs

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How often should I take my pug to the vet?**
Once a year for healthy adults. Twice a year after age 7. More frequently if managing any health conditions.

**Do all pugs need breathing surgery?**
Not all. Mild BAS can be managed with lifestyle adjustments. Moderate to severe cases benefit significantly from surgical correction. Have your vet assess your pug's airway during their first comprehensive exam.

**Are pugs always sick?**
No — many pugs live full, happy lives with minimal health interventions. The key is proactive management: healthy weight, daily wrinkle care, regular vet visits, and avoiding heat.

**What is the life expectancy of a pug?**
13–15 years with good care. Some pugs live to 16 or even 17.
