# How to Train a Pug: The Complete Training Guide for Stubborn (But Lovable) Dogs

Let's be honest about pugs: they are not Border Collies.

They will not hang on your every word. They will not sprint to you from across the yard on the first recall attempt. They will look at you, look at the treat, look at you again, and then sit down where they already are.

But here's what most people get wrong about pug training: pugs are actually quite intelligent. The issue isn't capability — it's motivation. Get the motivation right, and a pug can learn anything.

This guide covers everything you need to train your pug from puppy basics to advanced skills, including how to handle the most common behavior challenges.

---

## Understanding the Pug Brain

Before starting any training, understand what drives your pug:

1. **Food** — this is their primary currency. Use it.
2. **Praise** — pugs are deeply attached to their owners and crave approval
3. **Play** — less predictable than food but effective for some pugs

What does *not* work:
- Punishment or harsh corrections — pugs shut down and lose trust
- Long sessions — pugs bore quickly (5–10 minutes is the max)
- Repeating commands 10 times — say it once, wait, reward the correct response
- Training when they're hot, tired, or just ate — they'll be even less cooperative

The golden rule: **end every session on a success**, even if you have to make the last task very easy.

---

## When to Start Training

**Start the day you bring your pug home.** Whether they're 8 weeks or 8 years old.

Puppies under 16 weeks are in their critical socialization window — this is the best time to expose them to new people, sounds, environments, animals, and experiences. Everything they encounter positively during this period shapes their confidence for life.

Adult pugs are absolutely trainable. "Old dog, new tricks" is a myth. Adults often focus better than puppies because they have more impulse control.

## The 5 Essential Commands

Start with these before anything else:

### 1. Sit
- Hold a treat at your pug's nose
- Slowly move it backward over their head — their bottom will lower naturally
- The moment their bottom hits the floor, say "Sit" and give the treat
- Repeat 5–10 times per session
- Within a few sessions, try saying "Sit" before luring

### 2. Stay
- Ask for a sit
- Hold your palm out, say "Stay," and take one step back
- Return, treat and praise if they held position
- Gradually increase distance and duration
- Always return to your dog to release them — don't call them out of stay initially

### 3. Come (Recall)
- The most important safety command
- Start close (2–3 feet), crouch down, say your pug's name then "Come" in a happy voice
- Massive reward when they arrive — treats, praise, play
- Never call your pug to you for something unpleasant (bath, nail trim) — go get them instead
- Practice in enclosed spaces before trusting outdoors

### 4. Leave It
- Essential for pugs, who will eat anything
- Hold a treat in your closed fist
- Let your pug sniff and paw at it — say nothing
- The moment they pull away even slightly, say "Yes!" and give a *different* treat from your other hand
- Gradually progress to treats on the floor, then more tempting objects

### 5. Place/Bed
- Point to their bed, lure them onto it with a treat
- Say "Place" or "Bed" as they step onto it
- Treat generously while they stay there
- Useful for preventing begging at the table and creating a calm space

## Puppy-Specific Training

### House Training
Pugs are notoriously slow to house train — plan for 4–6 months of consistency.

**The routine that works:**
1. Take your pug outside immediately after waking, after eating, after playing, and every 1–2 hours
2. Go to the same spot each time
3. Wait quietly — no talking or playing until they eliminate
4. The moment they finish, **immediately** say "Good boy/girl!" and treat
5. If they have an accident inside, clean it thoroughly with an enzymatic cleaner (regular cleaners don't remove the scent cue that brings them back)

Never punish for accidents. They don't connect the punishment to the act — they just learn to be afraid of you.

### Crate Training
Crates are not cruel — they're a den. Most dogs feel safe in them once accustomed.

1. Leave the crate open with a blanket and toy inside — let your pug explore on their own
2. Start feeding meals near the crate, then inside it
3. Begin closing the door for short periods (1–2 minutes) while you're in the room
4. Gradually extend time and distance
5. Never use the crate as punishment

### Socialization
Expose your pug puppy to:
- People of all ages, sizes, and appearances
- Other dogs (vaccinated and calm)
- Traffic sounds, umbrellas, bicycles, skateboards
- Stairs, different floor surfaces, car rides
- Handling: touch ears, paws, mouth — this makes grooming and vet visits much easier

Puppy classes are excellent for structured socialization and basic training simultaneously.

## Common Behavior Problems and Solutions

### Pulling on the Leash
Pugs pull with surprising force for their size.

**Solution:**
- Stop walking the instant the leash tightens — become a statue
- Only move forward when the leash is loose
- Reward heavily for walking beside you
- Use a front-clip harness — it reduces pulling without harming their trachea (never use a neck collar for walks)

### Begging at the Table
Pugs are born performers. The moment someone laughs at their begging face and gives them a bite, you've created a habit that takes months to undo.

**Solution:**
- Feed your pug their meal before you eat yours — a full pug is a less persistent pug
- Train and consistently use the "Place" command during meals
- Everyone in the household must follow the same rule — one person giving in undoes everyone's work

### Separation Anxiety
Pugs bond intensely and can develop anxiety when alone.

**Prevention:**
- Practice short departures from puppyhood — step outside for 2 minutes, come back, no drama
- Leave gradually increasing alone time before requiring long separations
- Provide a food-stuffed toy (like a frozen Kong) when leaving

**If already present:**
- Consult your vet — anti-anxiety supplements or medication may help while you work on desensitization
- A dog trainer or behaviorist specializing in separation anxiety can create a systematic desensitization plan

### Jumping Up
Pugs jump to get closer to your face — it's affection, but it's also bad manners.

**Solution:**
- Turn your back and ignore completely when they jump
- Four paws on the floor = attention, pets, and praise
- Ask for a sit before greeting — redirect the excitement into something you can reward

## Training Schedule: 8 Weeks to 6 Months

| Age | Focus |
|---|---|
| 8–10 weeks | Socialization, name recognition, crate introduction, potty training |
| 10–12 weeks | Sit, come, handling exercises (ears, paws, mouth) |
| 12–16 weeks | Stay, leave it, leash introduction, puppy class |
| 4–6 months | Place, down, reinforcing all basics, longer stays, recall at distance |

Keep every session under 10 minutes. Multiple short sessions per day beat one long one.

## Advanced Training and Activities

Once basics are solid, pugs can excel at:

- **Trick training** — shake, roll over, spin, play dead. Pugs love performing and showing off.
- **Nosework** — using their sense of smell to find hidden objects. Mental stimulation without physical exertion — perfect for pugs.
- **Obedience classes** — structured class environments build focus around distractions
- **Therapy dog work** — pugs make exceptional therapy dogs; their affectionate, gentle nature is ideal for visiting hospitals, schools, and care homes

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are pugs hard to train?**
Not hard — just different. They need short, food-motivated, positive sessions. With the right approach, most pugs learn basics within a few weeks.

**At what age should I start training my pug?**
Immediately. The moment they come home. Even 8-week-old puppies can start learning their name, sit, and house training.

**Why does my pug ignore the "come" command?**
Usually because recall has been poisoned — it's been used for unpleasant things (bath, nail trim, end of play). Rebuild it from scratch with extremely high-value rewards and only positive associations.

**Can you train a pug not to snore?**
No — snoring is structural. If the snoring is very loud or your pug seems to struggle to breathe, that's a vet conversation, not a training issue.
